Introduction

Early in my academic career, I realized that I am driven to continuously expand my knowledge, and further, that I particularly enjoy the challenge of searching for answers to natural phenomena. I feel that these traits should prove particularly advantageous as I pursue my goal of becoming a research scientist. I am particularly passionate about research that sits at the interface of physics, biology, and technology. Although biological concepts are fascinating, I can only find true understanding by defining them in terms of mathematics, physical mechanics, fluid dynamics, coding, and chemistry.

During my undergraduate education I majored in Physics and Chemistry but maintained an interest in biology in hopes that I could eventually find a niche in which I could utilize my extensive training in physics and mathematics to investigate problems in complex biological systems. I identified a distinct interest in biomedical engineering, mathematical modeling, and medical physics; however, there were no resources for graduate study in these disciplines within my home country of Nepal. As a result, I emigrated to the United States to pursue a master’s degree in Physics at the University of Southern Mississippi in the lab of my thesis advisor, Dr. Ras B. Pandey. During my master’s work, I received significant training in polymer physics, material physics, and biophysics, as well as greater computational competency. My research with Dr. Pandey focused on the characterization of the transmembrane protein CorA and hHv1, using coarse-grained Monte Carlo simulation methods. My work also involved extensive use of coding for graphical analysis and visualization. In August of 2017, I successfully defended my thesis, entitled ‘Conformation of Transmembrane Segments of a Protein by a Coarse Grain Model’. My masters work resulted in the publication of three peer-reviewed articles.

After completing my master’s degree, I chose to pursue doctoral work in the Basic Medical Science Program at the University of South Alabama. The caliber of the faculty, the small student-to-faculty ratio, and the opportunity to receive a broad interdisciplinary foundation in biomedical science coursework were influential factors in my decision. However, the option of pursuing physiology and cell biology with a focus on the mechanical properties of cells seemed like a perfect fit for my interests and aptitudes. So, I chose to work with Dr. Stevens and Dr. Tambe to investigate the contribution of structural and mechanical stress to cell pathophysiology. In our lab, I am fortunate to have the opportunity to interact daily with collaborators specializing in lung physiology and pathophysiology, biomedical engineering, endothelial cell biology, physiology, protein structure, microbiology, biochemistry, and genetics. The ready availability of such a breadth of expertise and perspectives is, and will continue to be, paramount to the success of this project as well as my development as a scientist. With this vast environment, I have become increasingly proficient in the techniques and experimental design that have yielded the data currently serving as the foundation of this application.

I am making progress in my journey towards becoming a research scientist in the USA. I have completed the required coursework for my doctoral degree. I am gaining valuable experience in science writing and speaking. I have also had the opportunity to present my research at different national conferences. I participated in two weeks of summer short courses in clinical and translation research, organized by Center for Clinical and Translation Science, to strengthen my research skills. I trained on digital image analysis, which further added expertise in image analysis. In my research, I have made important observations about how substrate stiffness impacts the physical properties of endothelial cells, using novel approaches built in our lab. We have completed a study that examines the dynamic nature of endothelial cell-to-cell junctions, and we are now applying our novel approach to vascular segments in situ. I am excited to examine the nature of communication between mechanical forces and endothelial function in health and disease.

Outside my professional life, I am a wife of my handsome husband Durga P. Paudel (Ph.D in computational Physics and currently professor in Alabama School of Math and Science) and mother of two kids Denish Paudel (9 years) and Delisha Paudel (1 years). I love to travel all around the world with my family. I love walking in the natural trails and hiking.
